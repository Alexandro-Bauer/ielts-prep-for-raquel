Passage 1: The History of Tea

Tea is one of the world's oldest and most popular beverages, with origins tracing back to ancient China more than 5,000 years ago. According to legend, tea was discovered in 2737 BCE by the Chinese Emperor Shen Nong, who was boiling water when leaves from a nearby tea tree blew into his pot. The emperor was intrigued by the pleasant aroma and decided to taste the infusion, thus discovering tea's refreshing properties.

By the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE), tea had become China's national beverage and was considered essential to daily life. It was during this period that Lu Yu wrote the first definitive book on tea, "The Classic of Tea," which detailed cultivation methods, brewing techniques, and the cultural significance of tea in Chinese society. The Tang Dynasty also saw the development of tea as an item of trade, transported along the Silk Road to Tibet, the Middle East, and eventually to other parts of the world.

Tea arrived in Japan around the 6th century, brought by Buddhist monks who had traveled to China to study. The Japanese embraced tea culture enthusiastically, developing their own unique traditions, including the elaborate Japanese tea ceremony (chanoyu), which was perfected in the 16th century by the tea master Sen no Riky≈´. The ceremony emphasizes spiritual and aesthetic principles, viewing tea preparation as a meditative practice embodying harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility.

Europeans first encountered tea in the 16th century, with Portuguese merchants and missionaries bringing back accounts of the exotic beverage from their travels in the East. However, it wasn't until the 17th century that tea became popular in Europe, particularly in the Netherlands and England. The Dutch East India Company began importing tea to Europe in 1610, but it remained a luxury item due to its high cost, consumed primarily by the aristocracy and the wealthy merchant class.

Tea's journey to England is closely tied to Catherine of Braganza, a Portuguese princess who married King Charles II in 1662. Catherine was an avid tea drinker and introduced the beverage to the English court, making it fashionable among the nobility. By the 18th century, tea had become increasingly accessible to the middle classes, and tea houses began to spring up across London, serving as social hubs where people gathered to drink tea, exchange news, and conduct business.

The British East India Company, which had obtained a monopoly on tea imports to Britain, played a crucial role in expanding the tea trade. As demand for tea grew in Britain, the Company began to cultivate tea in India to reduce reliance on Chinese imports. The discovery that tea plants were native to Assam in India led to the establishment of tea plantations throughout the region in the 1830s. Later, tea cultivation expanded to Ceylon (now Sri Lanka) and parts of Africa, diversifying production beyond China.

The high taxes imposed on tea by the British government led to a thriving smuggling trade in the 18th century and played a role in the American Revolution. The infamous Boston Tea Party of 1773, in which American colonists dumped British tea into Boston Harbor to protest taxation without representation, was a pivotal event leading to American independence. Following these events, coffee temporarily surpassed tea as the preferred beverage in America.

Today, tea is the most widely consumed beverage in the world after water, with thousands of varieties available. Its global journey from a medicinal herb in ancient China to a staple beverage across cultures demonstrates tea's remarkable adaptability and enduring appeal. Each region has developed its own traditions around tea preparation and consumption, reflecting local tastes, customs, and values. Whether served hot or cold, plain or sweetened, in elaborate ceremonies or from disposable cups, tea continues to bring people together in a shared experience that transcends cultural boundaries.

Writing Test (60 minutes)

Writing Test Information

The writing test consists of two tasks:

  • Task 1 (20 minutes): Write a letter (General Training) or describe visual information (Academic)
  • Task 2 (40 minutes): Write an essay on a given topic

Complete both tasks within the 60-minute time limit. Task 2 is worth more marks than Task 1.

Writing Task 1 (General Training)

You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.

You recently stayed at a hotel and were very satisfied with the service. Write a letter to the hotel manager.

In your letter:

  • Say when you stayed at the hotel
  • Describe what you particularly liked about the hotel
  • Explain why you would recommend this hotel to others

Write at least 150 words.

Writing Task 2

You should spend about 40 minutes on this task.

Many people believe that international tourism is harmful to the environment and local cultures. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.

Write at least 250 words.

Speaking Test (11-14 minutes)

Speaking Test Information

The speaking test consists of three parts:

  • Part 1 (4-5 minutes): Introduction and questions on familiar topics
  • Part 2 (3-4 minutes): Individual long turn (1-2 minutes) with 1 minute preparation
  • Part 3 (4-5 minutes): Two-way discussion on more abstract aspects of Part 2 topic

For this practice test, you can record yourself responding to the questions and then review your answers.

Part 1: Introduction and general questions

Record yourself answering these questions as if you were speaking to an examiner:

1. Can you tell me your full name, please?

2. What should I call you?

3. Can I see your identification, please?

Topic: Home

4. Where do you live?

5. Do you live in a house or an apartment?

6. What do you like about living there?

7. Would you like to move to a different home in the future?

Topic: Free Time

8. What do you enjoy doing in your free time?

9. Do you have enough free time?

10. What activities would you like to try in the future?

Part 2: Individual long turn

You will have 1 minute to prepare your answer, and then you should speak for 1-2 minutes on this topic:

Describe a skill you would like to learn.

You should say:

  • what the skill is
  • why you want to learn it
  • how you would learn it

and explain how this skill would be useful in your life.

Part 3: Two-way discussion

Record yourself answering these follow-up questions related to skills and learning:

1. What skills do you think are most important for people to learn in today's world?

2. Do you think the skills people need now are different from those needed in the past? Why?

3. How has technology changed the way people learn new skills?

4. Do you think schools should focus more on practical skills or academic knowledge?

5. What's the best way for adults to continue learning throughout their lives?

6. How important is it for older people to learn new skills?
