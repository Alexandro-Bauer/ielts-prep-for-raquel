Welcome to Day 4 of your IELTS preparation! Today, we'll focus on two important components: Writing Task 2 essays and Speaking Part 2 (the "long turn").

Hour 1: Introduction to Writing Task 2

Writing Task 2 is an essay question that requires you to write at least 250 words in response to a point of view, argument, or problem. This task tests your ability to present and justify an opinion, discuss a problem, or present a solution.

IELTS Writing Task 2 Resources

Learn about IELTS Writing Task 2 structure and strategies:

  • IELTS.org - Writing Task 2 Format
  • IELTS Liz - Writing Task 2 Guide
  • E2 IELTS - Writing Task 2 Structure
  • IELTS Advantage - Writing Task 2 Tips

Types of Writing Task 2 Questions:

  1. Opinion Essays: Do you agree or disagree? / To what extent do you agree or disagree?
  2. Discussion Essays: Discuss both views and give your opinion
  3. Problem-Solution Essays: What are the problems and solutions?
  4. Advantages-Disadvantages Essays: What are the advantages and disadvantages?
  5. Double Question Essays: Two questions that need to be answered

Basic Essay Structure:

Four-Paragraph Structure:

Introduction:
  • Paraphrase the question (rewrite it in your own words)
  • State your position/thesis statement
Body Paragraph 1:
  • Topic sentence (main idea)
  • Explanation
  • Example
  • Supporting detail
Body Paragraph 2:
  • Topic sentence (second main idea)
  • Explanation
  • Example
  • Supporting detail
Conclusion:
  • Summarize main points
  • Restate your position
  • Final thought (optional)

Hour 2: Writing Task 2 - Practice and Analysis

Now let's look at a sample essay and analyze its structure and key elements.

Sample Essay Question:

Some people believe that all children should learn to play a musical instrument. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this view?

Sample Essay (Opinion Type):

In recent years, there has been considerable debate about whether all children should be required to learn a musical instrument as part of their education. While some people believe this should be mandatory, others think it should remain optional. This essay will discuss why I largely agree that learning a musical instrument offers significant benefits to children's development.


Learning to play a musical instrument provides numerous cognitive benefits for children. Studies have shown that music education enhances brain development, particularly in areas related to language processing and mathematical thinking. For example, children who regularly practice instruments like the piano or violin often demonstrate improved concentration, memory, and problem-solving skills in their academic subjects. Furthermore, these cognitive advantages can translate into better overall academic performance, which can benefit children throughout their education and into adulthood.


Beyond intellectual development, learning a musical instrument also cultivates important character traits and social skills. The process of mastering an instrument requires discipline, patience, and perseverance as children must practice regularly to improve their abilities. A child learning the guitar, for instance, must dedicate time each day to practice chords and techniques, teaching them the value of consistent effort. Additionally, playing music in groups, such as school orchestras or bands, helps children develop teamwork and communication skills as they learn to coordinate their playing with others and listen attentively.


In conclusion, I strongly agree that learning a musical instrument offers valuable benefits for children's intellectual and personal development. However, while I believe music education should be widely available and encouraged in schools, forcing every child to learn an instrument might not be practical or beneficial for all, as children have different interests and talents. Nevertheless, providing opportunities for musical education should be a priority in school curricula.

Essay Analysis:

  • Introduction:
    • Paraphrases the question
    • Clearly states the writer's position (largely agrees)
  • Body Paragraph 1:
    • Topic sentence about cognitive benefits
    • Explanation with specific examples
    • Supporting details about academic performance
  • Body Paragraph 2:
    • Topic sentence about character and social skills
    • Examples with specific details (guitar practice)
    • Additional point about group playing
  • Conclusion:
    • Restates position
    • Adds balanced view (not forcing every child)
    • Final recommendation

Practice Exercise: Writing an Introduction

Write an introduction paragraph for the following essay question:

"Some people believe that technology has made our lives too complicated. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Remember to include:

  • A paraphrase of the question
  • Your position (agree, disagree, or partially agree/disagree)

There is an ongoing debate about whether modern technology has made our day-to-day lives unnecessarily complex rather than simplifying them as intended. While technology has undoubtedly transformed how we work, communicate, and spend our leisure time, I partially agree with the view that it has added complications to our lives, although its benefits often outweigh these drawbacks.

Analysis:
  • The first sentence paraphrases the question using different vocabulary (ongoing debate, modern technology, unnecessarily complex)
  • The second sentence clearly states the writer's position (partially agree) and hints at the balanced approach the essay will take
  • The introduction is concise but informative, setting up the essay's direction

Key Writing Tips:

  • Always plan your essay before you start writing (spend 5 minutes planning)
  • Use a clear paragraph structure with topic sentences
  • Develop your ideas with examples and explanations
  • Use a range of vocabulary and grammatical structures
  • Stay on topic and answer the specific question asked
  • Aim for 250-280 words (writing too much may lead to errors)
  • Leave 2-3 minutes for checking your work

Hour 3: Speaking Part 2 - The Long Turn

Speaking Part 2 is often called the "long turn" because you need to speak for 1-2 minutes on a given topic. You'll receive a task card with a topic and several points to cover.

IELTS Speaking Part 2 Resources

Learn more about the Speaking Part 2 format and strategies:

  • IELTS.org - Speaking Part 2
  • IELTS Liz - Speaking Part 2 Guide
  • E2 IELTS - Speaking Part 2 Strategy
  • Fastrack IELTS - Speaking Part 2 Tips

Speaking Part 2 Format:

  1. You receive a task card with a topic and points to include
  2. You have 1 minute to prepare (make notes)
  3. You speak for 1-2 minutes on the topic
  4. The examiner may ask one brief question when you finish

Sample Task Card:

Describe a place you have visited that you found interesting.

You should say:

  • where the place is
  • when you visited this place
  • what you did there

and explain why you found this place interesting.

Preparation Strategy:

  • Use the 1-minute preparation time effectively:
    • Quickly choose a place (real or imagined)
    • Note down key points for each bullet point
    • Think of specific details and examples
  • Structure your answer:
    • Introduction: Briefly introduce the place
    • Main points: Cover each bullet point with details
    • Conclusion: Summarize why it was interesting

Practice Exercise: Note-Taking

You have 1 minute to make notes for the task card above. Write down key points that you would include in your answer:

Where: Auckland Domain park, central Auckland

When: Last summer, June 2024

These brief notes contain enough information to speak for 1-2 minutes. When speaking, you would expand on each point with details and personal observations.

Sample Response:

I'd like to talk about the Auckland Museum, which I visited last summer when I was exploring New Zealand. The museum is located in a beautiful neoclassical building in the Auckland Domain, which is a large park in the center of Auckland city.

I visited the museum in June last year during my holiday to New Zealand. It was actually a rainy day, so it was perfect timing to explore an indoor attraction. I spent nearly the whole day there because there was so much to see.

While I was there, I explored several fascinating exhibitions. The MƒÅori cultural galleries were particularly impressive, with elaborate carved meeting houses and waka (canoes) on display. I also spent time in the volcanic section, which was really relevant since Auckland is built on a field of dormant volcanoes. One of the highlights was attending a MƒÅori cultural performance, where I saw traditional songs and dances, including the famous haka. I also visited the war memorial sections which commemorate New Zealand's involvement in various conflicts.

I found this place incredibly interesting for several reasons. Firstly, it gave me a deep insight into New Zealand's history and culture, which I knew very little about before visiting. The MƒÅori artifacts were stunning and helped me understand the indigenous culture. I also appreciated the interactive displays that made learning more engaging - for example, there was a simulation of a volcanic eruption that was quite dramatic. Finally, the building itself is architecturally beautiful, and its position on a hill offers wonderful views of Auckland. Overall, the museum really enhanced my understanding of New Zealand and made my trip more meaningful.

Speaking Tips:

  • Speak for the full time - don't stop too early
  • Include specific details and personal experiences
  • Use a range of vocabulary and grammatical structures
  • Use linking words to connect your ideas (firstly, moreover, in addition)
  • Practice proper pronunciation and intonation
  • If you can't think of a real example, it's okay to invent one
